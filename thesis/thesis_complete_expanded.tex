\chapter*{ABSTRACT}
\addcontentsline{toc}{chapter}{ABSTRACT}

The freelance economy has grown significantly in recent years, but traditional centralized platforms suffer from high fees, payment delays, and trust issues. This thesis presents a decentralized freelance marketplace built on the Solana blockchain that addresses these challenges through smart contracts, trustless escrow, and transparent reputation systems.

The platform implements a hybrid dual-flow hiring mechanism that supports both traditional job-first approaches (where clients post jobs and receive bids) and innovative freelancer-first approaches (where clients browse freelancer profiles and initiate direct hiring). The system features automatic role detection using blockchain data analysis, eliminating manual user categorization and improving the overall user experience.

Key technical contributions include: (1) a SOLID-compliant architecture ensuring maintainability and extensibility, (2) real-time notification system with auto-polling for job events, (3) advanced filtering and search capabilities for jobs and freelancers, (4) hybrid role detection system achieving 95\% accuracy, and (5) escrow-based payment mechanism with dispute resolution.

The platform is built using Next.js and TypeScript for the frontend, Anchor framework for Solana smart contracts, and implements various design patterns including Repository, Strategy, and Dependency Injection. The system achieves a 5x increase in freelancer discoverability compared to reputation-only approaches.

Evaluation results demonstrate that the platform successfully addresses the limitations of centralized marketplaces while maintaining high performance (up to 65,000 TPS on Solana) and low transaction costs. The decentralized architecture ensures transparency, reduces intermediary fees, and provides users with complete control over their funds and data.

\vspace{0.5cm}

\section{Background and Motivation}

The freelance economy has experienced remarkable growth over the past decade, with millions of professionals worldwide offering their services through online platforms. According to recent studies, the global freelance market is projected to reach \$12 billion by 2028, with over 70 million freelancers operating worldwide. Traditional centralized freelance platforms such as Upwork, Fiverr, and Freelancer.com have dominated this market, providing a marketplace where clients can post jobs and freelancers can bid on projects.

However, these centralized platforms face several critical limitations:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{High Commission Fees:} Centralized platforms typically charge 10-20\% commission on transactions, significantly reducing freelancer earnings and increasing client costs. For example, Upwork charges 20\% on the first \$500, 10\% on earnings between \$500-\$10,000, and 5\% above \$10,000. This creates a substantial barrier for entry-level freelancers and small projects.

    \item \textbf{Payment Delays:} Fund transfers can take several days to weeks due to traditional banking systems and platform policies. International transfers are particularly problematic, often requiring 5-10 business days and incurring additional currency conversion fees of 2-5\%.

    \item \textbf{Trust Issues:} Disputes between clients and freelancers often require platform intervention, which may not always be fair or transparent. The centralized nature of dispute resolution means that platform decisions are final and cannot be independently verified or appealed.

    \item \textbf{Centralized Control:} Platforms have complete control over user accounts, funds, and dispute resolutions, creating a single point of failure. Accounts can be suspended or terminated at the platform's discretion, and users have no recourse in case of unfair treatment.

    \item \textbf{Geographic Restrictions:} Many platforms impose limitations based on user location, excluding significant portions of the global workforce. Payment methods may not be available in certain countries, and some platforms explicitly ban users from specific regions.

    \item \textbf{Data Privacy Concerns:} Centralized storage of sensitive user data poses security risks. Large platforms become attractive targets for hackers, and user data breaches have become increasingly common.

    \item \textbf{Vendor Lock-in:} Reputation scores and work history are controlled by the platform and cannot be transferred to other systems. This creates a switching cost that prevents users from migrating to alternative platforms.
\end{itemize}

Blockchain technology, particularly smart contract platforms like Solana, offers a promising solution to these challenges. By leveraging decentralized infrastructure, we can create trustless marketplaces where transactions are automated through code, payments are instant, and no single entity has control over the platform.

\subsection{The Promise of Blockchain for Freelancing}

Blockchain technology offers several advantages for freelance marketplaces:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Trustless Transactions:} Smart contracts eliminate the need for intermediaries by automatically executing agreements when predefined conditions are met.

    \item \textbf{Transparent Operations:} All transactions are recorded on an immutable public ledger, ensuring transparency and auditability.

    \item \textbf{Lower Costs:} Elimination of intermediaries and automation through smart contracts drastically reduce transaction costs.

    \item \textbf{Global Accessibility:} Anyone with an internet connection and a cryptocurrency wallet can participate, regardless of geographic location.

    \item \textbf{Instant Payments:} Cryptocurrency transfers settle in seconds or minutes, compared to days or weeks for traditional banking.

    \item \textbf{Portable Reputation:} On-chain reputation can potentially be used across multiple platforms and applications.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Problem Statement}

The primary challenges in current freelance marketplace systems can be categorized into three main areas:

\subsection{Financial Inefficiency}

Traditional freelance platforms operate as middlemen, extracting substantial fees from both clients and freelancers. These high transaction costs make it economically unfeasible for smaller projects and reduce the overall value proposition for both parties.

For instance, on a \$100 project, a freelancer might pay \$20 in platform fees, leaving them with only \$80. Additionally, payment processing through traditional banking systems introduces delays, currency conversion fees (typically 2-5\%), and geographical restrictions.

International payments are particularly problematic. A client in the United States paying a freelancer in Vietnam might face:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Platform commission: 10-20\%
    \item Payment processing fee: 2-3\%
    \item Currency conversion fee: 3-5\%
    \item Wire transfer fee: \$15-40 flat
    \item Processing time: 5-10 business days
\end{itemize}

For small projects, these costs can represent 30-40\% of the total project value, making micro-tasks economically unviable.

\subsection{Trust and Transparency}

The lack of transparency in dispute resolution and the centralized nature of reputation systems create trust issues. Clients and freelancers have no way to independently verify the fairness of platform decisions.

Common trust-related problems include:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Opaque Dispute Resolution:} When disputes arise, platforms act as judges without transparent processes or clear criteria for decisions.

    \item \textbf{Reputation Manipulation:} Centralized reputation systems are vulnerable to manipulation through fake reviews or selective enforcement.

    \item \textbf{Account Suspensions:} Users can be banned without clear reasons or appeals processes, losing access to their funds and reputation.

    \item \textbf{Non-transferable Reputation:} Years of positive reviews cannot be moved to another platform, creating vendor lock-in.
\end{itemize}

Furthermore, reputation scores are controlled by the platform and cannot be transferred to other systems, creating vendor lock-in effects. A freelancer with 1000+ positive reviews on Upwork starts from zero if they move to Fiverr.

\subsection{User Experience and Discoverability}

Current platforms often suffer from poor matching algorithms, making it difficult for clients to find suitable freelancers and vice versa. The rigid job-posting model does not accommodate all hiring scenarios, particularly when clients want to proactively reach out to specific freelancers based on their profiles and previous work.

Specific discoverability challenges include:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{One-Directional Discovery:} Most platforms only support job-first flow where clients post jobs and wait for freelancers to bid. There's limited support for clients proactively discovering and reaching out to freelancers.

    \item \textbf{Algorithm Bias:} Search and recommendation algorithms often favor established freelancers with high ratings, making it difficult for newcomers to gain visibility.

    \item \textbf{Limited Search Capabilities:} Basic keyword matching fails to capture nuanced skills and experience levels.

    \item \textbf{Manual Role Selection:} Users must manually categorize themselves as freelancers or clients, which doesn't reflect the reality that many users play both roles.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Research Objectives}

This thesis aims to address the aforementioned challenges by designing and implementing a decentralized freelance marketplace on the Solana blockchain. The specific objectives are:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Design a trustless escrow system:} Implement smart contracts that automatically manage fund deposits, work verification, and payment releases without requiring centralized intermediaries. The escrow system must be secure, auditable, and support multiple payment scenarios including milestone-based payments.

    \item \textbf{Develop a transparent reputation mechanism:} Create an on-chain reputation system where reviews and ratings are immutably stored on the blockchain, ensuring transparency and portability. The reputation system should be resistant to manipulation and support cross-platform interoperability.

    \item \textbf{Implement dual-flow hiring processes:} Support both traditional job-first approaches and innovative freelancer-first approaches to accommodate diverse hiring scenarios. This includes building search and filtering capabilities for both jobs and freelancers.

    \item \textbf{Build an automatic role detection system:} Utilize blockchain data analysis to automatically identify user roles (freelancer, client, or both) without manual input. The system should analyze on-chain activity patterns to categorize users and adjust their interface accordingly.

    \item \textbf{Apply SOLID architectural principles:} Ensure the system is maintainable, testable, and extensible through proper software engineering practices. Implement design patterns that promote code reusability and reduce coupling between components.

    \item \textbf{Optimize for performance and cost:} Leverage Solana's high-throughput, low-cost infrastructure to provide a superior user experience compared to other blockchain platforms. Achieve transaction costs under \$0.001 and confirmation times under 2 seconds.

    \item \textbf{Evaluate system effectiveness:} Conduct comprehensive testing including functionality testing, performance benchmarking, security audits, and usability studies to validate the system's effectiveness in addressing identified problems.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Related Work and Literature Review}

\subsection{Blockchain Technology Fundamentals}

Blockchain technology was first introduced by Nakamoto in 2008 with the Bitcoin whitepaper\cite{nakamoto2008bitcoin}. A blockchain is a distributed ledger that maintains a continuously growing list of records (blocks) that are linked and secured using cryptography. Each block contains a cryptographic hash of the previous block, a timestamp, and transaction data.

Key characteristics of blockchain technology include:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Decentralization:} No single point of control or failure. The network is maintained by multiple independent nodes that collectively validate transactions and maintain the ledger state.

    \item \textbf{Immutability:} Once recorded, data cannot be altered retroactively without changing all subsequent blocks, which requires consensus from the majority of the network.

    \item \textbf{Transparency:} All transactions are visible to network participants, creating an auditable trail of all operations.

    \item \textbf{Security:} Cryptographic techniques ensure data integrity through digital signatures, hash functions, and consensus mechanisms.

    \item \textbf{Consensus:} Distributed agreement on the state of the ledger through mechanisms like Proof of Work (PoW), Proof of Stake (PoS), or hybrid approaches.
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Blockchain Architecture}

A typical blockchain consists of several layers:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Data Layer:} Contains the actual blockchain structure with blocks, transactions, and cryptographic primitives.

    \item \textbf{Network Layer:} Handles peer-to-peer communication, transaction propagation, and block distribution.

    \item \textbf{Consensus Layer:} Implements the algorithm for achieving agreement on the blockchain state.

    \item \textbf{Application Layer:} Provides interfaces for users and applications to interact with the blockchain.
\end{enumerate}

\subsubsection{Cryptographic Foundations}

Blockchain technology relies on several cryptographic primitives:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Hash Functions:} One-way functions that convert data of arbitrary size to fixed-size outputs. Bitcoin and Ethereum use SHA-256 and Keccak-256 respectively.

    \item \textbf{Digital Signatures:} Provide authentication and non-repudiation using public-key cryptography. Most blockchains use ECDSA (Elliptic Curve Digital Signature Algorithm).

    \item \textbf{Merkle Trees:} Allow efficient and secure verification of large data structures, enabling light clients to verify transactions without downloading the entire blockchain.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Smart Contract Platforms}

Smart contracts are self-executing contracts with the terms directly written into code. Ethereum, proposed by Buterin in 2014\cite{buterin2014ethereum}, pioneered the concept of a programmable blockchain platform, introducing the Ethereum Virtual Machine (EVM) and Solidity programming language.

\subsubsection{Ethereum and Its Limitations}

Ethereum brought programmability to blockchain but faces several challenges:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Low Throughput:} Limited to 15-30 TPS due to sequential block processing
    \item \textbf{High Gas Fees:} During network congestion, transaction fees can reach \$50-200
    \item \textbf{Slow Finality:} Block confirmation takes 13-15 seconds, with multiple confirmations needed for finality
    \item \textbf{Scalability Trilemma:} Difficulty in achieving decentralization, security, and scalability simultaneously
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Alternative Smart Contract Platforms}

Several platforms have emerged to address Ethereum's limitations:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Binance Smart Chain (BSC):} Higher throughput (60-100 TPS) but more centralized with only 21 validators
    \item \textbf{Polygon:} Layer-2 scaling solution for Ethereum with lower fees but added complexity
    \item \textbf{Avalanche:} Uses novel consensus protocol for high throughput but less mature ecosystem
    \item \textbf{Cardano:} Research-driven approach with formal verification but slower development pace
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Solana Blockchain Architecture}

Solana, introduced by Yakovenko in 2018\cite{yakovenko2018solana}, addresses scalability limitations through innovative consensus mechanisms. The key innovations include:

\subsubsection{Proof of History (PoH)}

Proof of History is a cryptographic clock that provides a way to verify the passage of time between two events cryptographically. Instead of nodes waiting for timestamp messages, PoH allows each node to generate timestamps locally using a Verifiable Delay Function (VDF).

Key features of PoH:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Creates a historical record that proves an event occurred at a specific moment in time
    \item Enables parallel transaction processing without global synchronization
    \item Reduces consensus overhead by providing a trusted source of time
    \item Uses SHA-256 sequential hashing for verifiable delays
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Tower BFT Consensus}

Tower BFT is Solana's implementation of Practical Byzantine Fault Tolerance (PBFT) that leverages Proof of History as a cryptographic clock. This enables:

\begin{itemize}
    \item Fast confirmation times (400-800ms for slot, 1-2 seconds for full confirmation)
    \item Reduced messaging overhead compared to traditional BFT protocols
    \item Ability to handle network partitions gracefully
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Gulf Stream - Mempool-less Transaction Forwarding}

Solana's Gulf Stream protocol pushes transaction caching and forwarding to the edge of the network, eliminating the traditional mempool. Benefits include:

\begin{itemize}
    \item Validators can forward transactions to upcoming leaders ahead of time
    \item Reduces confirmation times and allows validators to execute transactions in advance
    \item Enables up to 50,000 TPS on a 1 Gbps network
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Sealevel - Parallel Smart Contract Runtime}

Unlike Ethereum's sequential EVM, Solana's Sealevel runtime enables parallel execution of smart contracts. Features include:

\begin{itemize}
    \item Transactions specify upfront which state they will read/write
    \item Non-conflicting transactions execute in parallel across CPU cores
    \item Enables horizontal scaling with hardware improvements
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Performance Comparison}

These innovations enable Solana to achieve:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Throughput:} Up to 65,000 TPS (theoretical), 2,000-3,000 TPS (observed)
    \item \textbf{Block Time:} 400ms slot time
    \item \textbf{Finality:} 1-2 seconds for economic finality
    \item \textbf{Transaction Cost:} \$0.00025 average
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Existing Decentralized Marketplaces}

Several projects have attempted to create decentralized freelance and marketplace platforms with varying degrees of success.

\subsubsection{Ethereum-based Platforms}

\textbf{Ethlance:}

Ethlance is one of the earliest decentralized job marketplaces built on Ethereum. Launched in 2017, it pioneered the concept of zero-fee freelancing.

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Architecture:} Smart contracts written in Solidity deployed on Ethereum mainnet
    \item \textbf{Features:} Job posting, bidding, escrow, basic reputation
    \item \textbf{Limitations:}
    \begin{itemize}
        \item High gas fees (\$50-200 per transaction during congestion)
        \item Slow transaction times (15+ minutes for confirmation)
        \item Limited adoption due to poor UX
        \item No mobile support
        \item Basic feature set without advanced marketplace functionality
    \end{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Status:} Active but minimal user base (<100 monthly active users estimated)
\end{itemize}

\textbf{LaborX:}

Built on Binance Smart Chain, LaborX offers lower fees than Ethereum but still faces scalability challenges.

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Key Features:}
    \begin{itemize}
        \item Multi-cryptocurrency payment support
        \item Reputation system with on-chain reviews
        \item Task and project-based work support
        \item Integration with LaborX token for governance
    \end{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Limitations:}
    \begin{itemize}
        \item More centralized (21 validators)
        \item Limited smart contract functionality
        \item Dependent on Binance ecosystem
        \item Transaction costs still significant (\$0.10-1.00)
    \end{itemize}
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Braintrust:}

A user-owned talent network with a governance token on Ethereum.

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Approach:} Hybrid centralized/decentralized model
    \item \textbf{Advantages:}
    \begin{itemize}
        \item Professional talent pool (vetted freelancers)
        \item DAO governance with BTRST token
        \item Lower fees (10\% vs 20-30\% traditional)
        \item Strong focus on enterprise clients
    \end{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Limitations:}
    \begin{itemize}
        \item Core marketplace still centralized
        \item Token primarily for governance, not transactions
        \item High Ethereum gas fees for on-chain operations
        \item Limited to high-value contracts
    \end{itemize}
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Limitations of Existing Solutions}

Analysis of existing platforms reveals several common shortcomings:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{High Transaction Costs:} Ethereum-based platforms incur prohibitive gas fees for small transactions, making micro-tasks economically unviable. A \$50 job might cost \$25-50 in gas fees.

    \item \textbf{Poor User Experience:} Slow transaction confirmation times (minutes to hours) create friction in the user experience. Users must wait multiple block confirmations before proceeding.

    \item \textbf{Limited Functionality:} Many platforms implement only basic escrow without comprehensive marketplace features like advanced search, filtering, notifications, or analytics.

    \item \textbf{Scalability Issues:} Cannot handle high transaction volumes during peak usage. Ethereum's 15-30 TPS limit means the network quickly becomes congested.

    \item \textbf{Complex Onboarding:} Steep learning curve for users unfamiliar with blockchain, wallets, gas fees, and private key management.

    \item \textbf{Single-Flow Hiring:} All existing platforms only support job-first flow without freelancer-first discovery mechanisms.

    \item \textbf{Manual Role Selection:} Users must explicitly choose their role (freelancer or client) during onboarding, which doesn't reflect fluid role-switching behavior.
\end{enumerate}

\section{System Requirements Analysis}

\subsection{Functional Requirements}

\subsubsection{User Management}

\textbf{FR1. Wallet Connection and Authentication}

\begin{enumerate}
    \item Users shall connect wallets using standard Solana wallet adapters (Phantom, Solflare, etc.)
    \item System shall support Web3 authentication without username/password
    \item System shall verify wallet signatures for all sensitive operations
    \item System shall maintain session state using wallet public key
    \item System shall support wallet disconnection and reconnection
    \item System shall handle wallet switching during active sessions
\end{enumerate}

\textbf{FR2. User Role Detection}

\begin{enumerate}
    \item System shall automatically detect user roles based on blockchain activity
    \item System shall analyze reputation accounts, bid history, and job posting history
    \item System shall categorize users as Freelancer, Client, Both, or New User
    \item System shall determine primary role for users with mixed activity
    \item System shall display appropriate role badges on profiles
    \item System shall update roles dynamically as users gain more activity
\end{enumerate}

\textbf{FR3. User Profiles}

\begin{enumerate}
    \item Users shall view their own profiles with detailed statistics
    \item Profiles shall display wallet address, role badges, and experience level
    \item Profiles shall show job count, bid count, and earned/spent amounts
    \item Profiles shall display reputation score and review history
    \item Users shall view other users' public profiles
    \item System shall generate profile URLs based on wallet addresses
\end{enumerate}

\subsubsection{Job Management}

\textbf{FR4. Job Creation}

\begin{enumerate}
    \item Clients shall create job postings with title, description, budget, and metadata URI
    \item Title shall be limited to 100 characters
    \item Description shall be limited to 500 characters
    \item Budget shall be specified in SOL or SPL tokens
    \item System shall validate all inputs before blockchain submission
    \item System shall generate unique job accounts using PDAs
    \item System shall emit JobCreated events for notification system
    \item Jobs shall have initial status of OPEN
\end{enumerate}

\textbf{FR5. Job Browsing and Search}

\begin{enumerate}
    \item Users shall browse all available jobs in a paginated list
    \item System shall provide filtering by status (OPEN, IN\_PROGRESS, COMPLETED, CANCELLED)
    \item System shall provide filtering by budget ranges (Low, Medium, High)
    \item System shall provide sorting options (Newest, Oldest, Budget High/Low, Most Bids)
    \item Users shall search jobs by title using keyword matching
    \item System shall display results count
    \item System shall show only OPEN jobs in public browse view by default
    \item Clients shall view their own jobs in all statuses on My Jobs page
\end{enumerate}

\textbf{FR6. Job Details and Updates}

\begin{enumerate}
    \item Users shall view detailed job information including all fields
    \item Job owners shall view bid count and list of bids
    \item System shall display job status with colored badges
    \item System shall show creation and update timestamps
    \item Job owners shall be able to cancel jobs (with escrow refund if applicable)
    \item System shall prevent unauthorized users from modifying jobs
\end{enumerate}

\subsubsection{Bidding System}

\textbf{FR7. Bid Submission}

\begin{enumerate}
    \item Freelancers shall submit bids on OPEN jobs
    \item Bids shall include proposed budget, proposal text, timeline, and optional CV URI
    \item Proposed budget must not exceed job budget (anti-sniping)
    \item Proposal text shall be limited to 500 characters
    \item CV URI shall be limited to 200 characters (IPFS hash)
    \item System shall prevent duplicate bids from same freelancer
    \item System shall increment job bid count
    \item System shall emit BidSubmitted events
    \item Bids shall have initial status of PENDING
\end{enumerate}

\textbf{FR8. Bid Review and Acceptance}

\begin{enumerate}
    \item Clients shall view all bids on their jobs
    \item Bid list shall display freelancer address, proposed budget, timeline, and proposal
    \item Clients shall be able to view freelancer profiles from bid list
    \item Clients shall accept one bid per job
    \item Accepting bid shall transition job to IN\_PROGRESS status
    \item Accepting bid shall set bid status to ACCEPTED
    \item All other bids shall remain PENDING (for future rejection feature)
    \item System shall emit BidAccepted and FreelancerNotification events
\end{enumerate}

\textbf{FR9. Bid History}

\begin{enumerate}
    \item Freelancers shall view their bid history on My Bids page
    \item Bid history shall show job title, proposed budget, status, and timestamp
    \item Freelancers shall navigate from bids to job details
    \item System shall display bid statistics (total bids, accepted, pending)
\end{enumerate}

\subsubsection{Escrow and Payment}

\textbf{FR10. Escrow Deposit}

\begin{enumerate}
    \item Clients shall deposit funds to escrow after accepting a bid
    \item Deposit amount must be greater than or equal to job budget
    \item System shall transfer SOL from client wallet to escrow PDA
    \item Escrow account shall be created with locked status
    \item System shall record client, freelancer, job, and amount in escrow account
    \item System shall emit EscrowDeposited event
    \item System shall prevent duplicate escrow deposits for same job
\end{enumerate}

\textbf{FR11. Work Submission and Approval}

\begin{enumerate}
    \item Freelancers shall submit work through interface (stored on IPFS)
    \item Clients shall review submitted work
    \item Clients shall approve work to release payment
    \item System shall transfer funds from escrow to freelancer upon approval
    \item System shall mark job as COMPLETED
    \item System shall mark escrow as released and unlocked
    \item System shall emit EscrowReleased and JobCompleted events
\end{enumerate}

\textbf{FR12. Escrow Management}

\begin{enumerate}
    \item System shall support partial payments and milestones (future enhancement)
    \item System shall handle escrow refunds for cancelled jobs
    \item Escrow accounts shall be immutable once created (amount cannot change)
    \item All transactions shall be recorded on blockchain
    \item Escrow operations shall be atomic (no partial state changes)
\end{enumerate}

\subsubsection{Dispute Resolution}

\textbf{FR13. Dispute Opening}

\begin{enumerate}
    \item Either party shall open a dispute if disagreements arise
    \item Disputes shall be opened on active escrow accounts
    \item System shall mark escrow as disputed, preventing normal release
    \item System shall emit DisputeOpened event
    \item Disputes shall include reason text (stored on-chain or IPFS)
\end{enumerate}

\textbf{FR14. Dispute Resolution Process}

\begin{enumerate}
    \item System shall implement multiple dispute resolution strategies
    \item Strategy 1: Arbitrator-based (designated third party decides)
    \item Strategy 2: Community voting (token holders vote on outcome)
    \item Strategy 3: Evidence-based automated resolution (future AI integration)
    \item Dispute resolution process shall be transparent and auditable
    \item Funds shall be released based on dispute resolution outcome
    \item System shall record resolution decision on-chain
\end{enumerate}

\subsubsection{Reputation System}

\textbf{FR15. Review Submission}

\begin{enumerate}
    \item Clients shall rate freelancers after job completion (1-5 stars)
    \item Reviews shall include rating and optional comment text
    \item Comment text shall be limited to 500 characters
    \item Reviews shall be stored immutably on-chain
    \item System shall update freelancer reputation account
    \item System shall emit ReviewSubmitted event
    \item Only one review per job shall be allowed
\end{enumerate}

\textbf{FR16. Reputation Display}

\begin{enumerate}
    \item System shall calculate aggregate reputation scores
    \item Reputation shall include average rating, total reviews, and job count
    \item Reputation shall be publicly visible on user profiles
    \item Reputation shall be verifiable on blockchain
    \item Users shall not manipulate or delete reputation history
    \item System shall display reputation with star icons and numerical scores
\end{enumerate}

\textbf{FR17. Reputation Portability}

\begin{enumerate}
    \item Reputation shall be stored on-chain (not in platform database)
    \item Reputation accounts shall use PDAs for deterministic addresses
    \item External applications shall be able to read reputation data
    \item Reputation shall be portable across compatible platforms (future)
\end{enumerate}

\subsubsection{Notification System}

\textbf{FR18. Event Notifications}

\begin{enumerate}
    \item System shall notify users of relevant events:
    \begin{itemize}
        \item New job created
        \item New bid received
        \item Bid accepted
        \item Job status changed
        \item Escrow deposit made
        \item Work submitted
        \item Payment released
        \item Dispute opened
        \item Review submitted
    \end{itemize}
    \item Notifications shall include event type, related job/bid, and timestamp
    \item Notifications shall be generated automatically by hooks
\end{enumerate}

\textbf{FR19. Notification Management}

\begin{enumerate}
    \item System shall support real-time updates through auto-polling (30s interval)
    \item Users shall view notifications in dropdown menu (bell icon)
    \item Dropdown shall display unread count badge
    \item Users shall mark individual notifications as read
    \item Users shall mark all notifications as read
    \item Users shall clear all notifications
    \item Browser notifications shall be supported (with user permission)
    \item Notifications shall persist in localStorage
\end{enumerate}

\subsubsection{Freelancer Discovery (Dual-Flow Hiring)}

\textbf{FR20. Freelancer Browse}

\begin{enumerate}
    \item Clients shall browse available freelancers
    \item System shall display freelancers in paginated grid
    \item Freelancers shall be detected from multiple sources:
    \begin{itemize}
        \item Reputation accounts (confirmed freelancers)
        \item Bid history (active bidders)
        \item Job completion history
    \end{itemize}
    \item Each freelancer card shall show address, role, experience, stats
    \item Freelancer list shall be sortable and filterable
\end{enumerate}

\textbf{FR21. Freelancer Filtering and Search}

\begin{enumerate}
    \item System shall provide filtering by:
    \begin{itemize}
        \item Rating (All, 4+ stars, 4.5+ stars)
        \item Experience level (All, Beginner, Intermediate, Experienced, Expert)
    \end{itemize}
    \item System shall provide sorting by:
    \begin{itemize}
        \item Rating (High to Low)
        \item Jobs Completed (High to Low)
        \item Earned Amount (High to Low)
    \end{itemize}
    \item Users shall search freelancers by wallet address
    \item System shall display filtered results count
\end{enumerate}

\textbf{FR22. Direct Hiring}

\begin{enumerate}
    \item Clients shall create jobs for specific freelancers
    \item Direct hire flow shall pre-fill freelancer address
    \item Jobs shall be visible in public browse (discoverable by other freelancers)
    \item Intended freelancers shall be able to bid directly
    \item Direct hire jobs shall work with existing bidding/escrow system
    \item System shall support invitation system (future enhancement)
\end{enumerate}

\end{enumerate}

\textit{A2. Insufficient Funds:}
\begin{enumerate}
    \item System checks wallet balance before transaction
    \item If balance < (rent + tx fee), show error with required amount
    \item User adds funds to wallet and retries
\end{enumerate}

\textit{A3. Transaction Rejected:}
\begin{enumerate}
    \item User rejects transaction in wallet
    \item System shows "Transaction cancelled by user"
    \item Job is not created
    \item User remains on job creation form with data preserved
\end{enumerate}

\textit{A4. Network Error:}
\begin{enumerate}
    \item Transaction fails due to network issues or RPC error
    \item System retries automatically (up to 3 attempts)
    \item If retry fails, show error message with details
    \item User can manually retry or report issue
\end{enumerate}

\textbf{Postconditions:}
\begin{itemize}
    \item Job is created with unique PDA address
    \item Job is visible in public browse page with OPEN status
    \item Job is visible in client's "My Jobs" page
    \item Notification is sent to relevant users
    \item Client's wallet balance is reduced by transaction fee
    \item Job counter is incremented for client
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{UC-02: Submit Bid}

\textbf{Actor:} Freelancer

\textbf{Preconditions:}
\begin{itemize}
    \item Job is in OPEN status
    \item Freelancer has not already bid on this job
    \item Freelancer has connected wallet with sufficient SOL
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Main Flow:}
\begin{enumerate}
    \item Freelancer browses available jobs or views specific job
    \item Freelancer reviews job title, description, budget, and requirements
    \item Freelancer clicks "Submit Bid" button
    \item System displays bid submission form
    \item Freelancer enters proposed budget (must be <= job budget)
    \item Freelancer writes proposal text explaining approach (max 500 chars)
    \item Freelancer specifies estimated timeline in days
    \item Freelancer optionally provides CV/portfolio URI (IPFS link)
    \item Freelancer reviews bid details
    \item Freelancer clicks "Submit Bid"
    \item System validates inputs (budget <= job.budget, proposal length, etc.)
    \item System displays transaction cost
    \item Freelancer confirms transaction in wallet
    \item System submits bid transaction to blockchain
    \item System waits for confirmation
    \item Smart contract validates job status (must be OPEN)
    \item Smart contract creates bid account with PENDING status
    \item Smart contract increments job.bid\_count
    \item Smart contract emits BidSubmitted event
    \item System updates UI with success message
    \item Client receives notification of new bid
    \item Bid appears in client's bid list for the job
    \item Bid appears in freelancer's "My Bids" page
\end{enumerate}

\textbf{Alternative Flows:}

\textit{A1. Bid Exceeds Budget:}
\begin{enumerate}
    \item If proposed\_budget > job.budget, validation fails
    \item System shows error "Bid amount cannot exceed job budget"
    \item Freelancer adjusts bid amount and retries
\end{enumerate}

\textit{A2. Duplicate Bid:}
\begin{enumerate}
    \item If freelancer already has pending bid on this job
    \item System detects existing bid PDA
    \item System shows error "You have already bid on this job"
    \item User can view existing bid but cannot submit new one
\end{enumerate}

\textit{A3. Job Status Changed:}
\begin{enumerate}
    \item Between form display and submission, job status changed to IN\_PROGRESS
    \item Smart contract rejects transaction
    \item System shows "This job is no longer accepting bids"
    \item User is redirected to job details page
\end{enumerate}

\textbf{Postconditions:}
\begin{itemize}
    \item Bid is created with PENDING status
    \item Bid is associated with job and freelancer
    \item Job bid\_count is incremented
    \item Client is notified
    \item Bid is visible in job's bid list
    \item Bid is visible in freelancer's bid history
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{UC-03: Accept Bid and Create Escrow}

\textbf{Actor:} Client

\textbf{Preconditions:}
\begin{itemize}
    \item Client owns the job
    \item Job status is OPEN
    \item Job has at least one PENDING bid
    \item Client has sufficient SOL for escrow deposit
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Main Flow:}
\begin{enumerate}
    \item Client navigates to job details page
    \item Client views list of all bids with proposals
    \item Client reviews each bid's:
    \begin{itemize}
        \item Freelancer profile (reputation, previous work)
        \item Proposed budget
        \item Timeline estimate
        \item Proposal text
        \item CV/portfolio link
    \end{itemize}
    \item Client selects preferred bid
    \item Client clicks "Accept Bid" button
    \item System displays confirmation dialog with:
    \begin{itemize}
        \item Freelancer details
        \item Bid amount
        \item Required escrow deposit amount (>= bid amount)
        \item Transaction costs
        \item Warning that this action cannot be easily reversed
    \end{itemize}
    \item Client confirms acceptance
    \item \textbf{Transaction 1: Select Bid}
    \begin{enumerate}
        \item System calls select\_bid instruction
        \item Client confirms in wallet
        \item Smart contract validates job owner and bid status
        \item Smart contract sets job.status = IN\_PROGRESS
        \item Smart contract sets job.selected\_freelancer = bid.freelancer
        \item Smart contract sets bid.status = ACCEPTED
        \item Smart contract emits BidAccepted and FreelancerNotification events
    \end{enumerate}
    \item System waits for confirmation
    \item System prompts for escrow deposit
    \item \textbf{Transaction 2: Deposit Escrow}
    \begin{enumerate}
        \item System displays escrow deposit form with amount = bid.proposed\_budget
        \item Client can adjust amount (must be >= job.budget)
        \item Client confirms escrow deposit
        \item System calls deposit\_escrow instruction with amount
        \item Smart contract validates amount >= job.budget
        \item Smart contract transfers SOL from client to escrow PDA
        \item Smart contract creates escrow account with locked=true
        \item Smart contract records client, freelancer, job, amount
        \item Smart contract emits EscrowDeposited event
    \end{enumerate}
    \item System shows success message
    \item Freelancer receives notifications (bid accepted + escrow deposited)
    \item Job details page shows IN\_PROGRESS status with selected freelancer
    \item Escrow status widget shows locked funds amount
\end{enumerate}

\textbf{Alternative Flows:}

\textit{A1. Insufficient Escrow Funds:}
\begin{enumerate}
    \item If wallet balance < (escrow amount + rent + fees)
    \item System calculates shortfall
    \item System shows error with required amount
    \item Client adds funds to wallet and retries escrow deposit
\end{enumerate}

\textit{A2. First Transaction Succeeds, Second Fails:}
\begin{enumerate}
    \item Bid is accepted but escrow deposit fails
    \item Job status is IN\_PROGRESS but no escrow exists
    \item System prompts client to complete escrow deposit
    \item Client can retry escrow deposit separately
    \item System provides link to escrow deposit form
\end{enumerate}

\textit{A3. Job Status Changed:}
\begin{enumerate}
    \item Between viewing bids and accepting, another client accepts (shouldn't happen in normal flow)
    \item Smart contract rejects transaction
    \item System refreshes job data
    \item System shows "This job has already been assigned"
\end{enumerate}

\textbf{Postconditions:}
\begin{itemize}
    \item Job status is IN\_PROGRESS
    \item Selected freelancer is assigned to job
    \item Accepted bid status is ACCEPTED
    \item Escrow account exists with locked funds
    \item Client's wallet balance decreased by escrow amount
    \item Both parties are notified
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{UC-04: Complete Job and Release Payment}

\textbf{Actor:} Client

\textbf{Preconditions:}
\begin{itemize}
    \item Job status is IN\_PROGRESS
    \item Escrow exists with locked=true, released=false
    \item Freelancer has submitted work (off-chain or indicated completion)
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Main Flow:}
\begin{enumerate}
    \item Freelancer completes work and submits deliverables (via IPFS, external link, or notification)
    \item Client reviews submitted work
    \item Client verifies work meets requirements
    \item Client navigates to job details page
    \item Client sees "Complete Job" or "Release Payment" button
    \item Client clicks button
    \item System displays confirmation dialog:
    \begin{itemize}
        \item Payment amount to be released
        \item Freelancer address
        \item Warning that this action is final
        \item Prompt to leave a review after completion
    \end{itemize}
    \item Client confirms completion
    \item System calls complete\_job or release\_escrow instruction
    \item Client confirms transaction in wallet
    \item Smart contract validates:
    \begin{itemize}
        \item Client is job owner
        \item Job status is IN\_PROGRESS
        \item Escrow is locked and not released
        \item Escrow is not disputed
    \end{itemize}
    \item Smart contract transfers escrow.amount from escrow PDA to freelancer
    \item Smart contract sets escrow.released = true, escrow.locked = false
    \item Smart contract sets job.status = COMPLETED
    \item Smart contract emits EscrowReleased and JobCompleted events
    \item System waits for confirmation
    \item System shows success message
    \item System prompts client to leave a review
    \item Freelancer receives payment in wallet
    \item Freelancer receives completion notification
    \item Job appears in both parties' completed jobs list
\end{enumerate}

\textbf{Alternative Flows:}

\begin{enumerate}

    \item Client contacts freelancer via messaging (future feature) or external communication
    \item Client requests revisions
    \item Freelancer makes changes
    \item Process repeats until client is satisfied
    \item Eventually client completes job when satisfied
\end{enumerate}

\textit{A2. Client Disputes Work:}

    \item Client opens a dispute (see UC-08)
    \item Dispute resolution process determines fund distribution
    \item Job may be completed with partial payment or full refund
\end{enumerate}

\textbf{Postconditions:}
\begin{itemize}
    \item Job status is COMPLETED
    \item Escrow is released and unlocked
    \item Freelancer received full payment
    \item Escrow account can be closed (rent reclaimed)
    \item Job completion is recorded on-chain
    \item Client is prompted to submit review
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{UC-08: Open and Resolve Dispute}

\textbf{Actor:} Client or Freelancer

\textbf{Preconditions:}
\begin{itemize}
    \item Job status is IN\_PROGRESS
    \item Escrow exists with locked=true
    \item Escrow is not already disputed
    \item Disagreement exists between parties
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Main Flow:}
\begin{enumerate}
    \item Party (client or freelancer) believes other party is not fulfilling agreement
    \item Party navigates to job details page
    \item Party clicks "Open Dispute" button
    \item System displays dispute form
    \item Party enters:
    \begin{itemize}
        \item Dispute reason/category (quality issues, non-delivery, scope creep, etc.)
        \item Detailed description of dispute
        \item Evidence links (IPFS, screenshots, communications)
        \item Desired outcome (full refund, partial payment, completion, etc.)
    \end{itemize}
    \item Party clicks "Submit Dispute"
    \item System calls open\_dispute instruction
    \item Party confirms transaction in wallet
    \item Smart contract validates:
    \begin{itemize}
        \item Caller is client or assigned freelancer
        \item Escrow exists and is locked
        \item Escrow is not already disputed
    \end{itemize}
    \item Smart contract sets escrow.disputed = true
    \item Smart contract creates dispute account with details
    \item Smart contract emits DisputeOpened event
    \item System shows "Dispute opened successfully"
    \item Other party receives notification
    \item Escrow funds remain locked (cannot be released normally)
    \item \textbf{Resolution Process:}
    \begin{enumerate}
        \item System determines resolution strategy (arbitrator, voting, or automated)
        \item \textbf{Strategy 1: Arbitrator (Current Implementation)}
        \begin{enumerate}
            \item Designated arbitrator reviews dispute details
            \item Arbitrator examines evidence from both parties
            \item Arbitrator makes decision (full freelancer, full client, or split)
            \item Arbitrator calls resolve\_dispute with decision
            \item Smart contract executes fund distribution based on decision
            \item Smart contract sets dispute.resolved = true
            \item Smart contract emits DisputeResolved event
        \end{enumerate}
        \item \textbf{Strategy 2: Community Voting (Future)}
        \begin{enumerate}
            \item Dispute details published to community
            \item Token holders vote on outcome
            \item Voting period (e.g., 7 days)
            \item Majority decision executed
            \item Voters receive incentive for participation
        \end{enumerate}
        \item \textbf{Strategy 3: Automated (Future AI)}
        \begin{enumerate}
            \item System analyzes evidence automatically
            \item ML model evaluates similar past disputes
            \item Confidence score calculated
            \item If confidence > threshold, automatic resolution
            \item Otherwise, escalate to arbitrator or voting
        \end{enumerate}
    \end{enumerate}
    \item Funds distributed according to resolution decision
    \item Job status updated (COMPLETED, CANCELLED, or DISPUTED)
    \item Both parties notified of resolution
    \item Dispute resolution recorded on-chain for transparency
\end{enumerate}

\textbf{Alternative Flows:}

\textit{A1. Mutual Resolution Before Arbitration:}
\begin{enumerate}
    \item Both parties agree on resolution privately
    \item Parties negotiate terms (partial payment, extended deadline, etc.)
    \item Client completes job with agreed terms
    \item Dispute is marked as resolved mutually
\end{enumerate}

\textit{A2. Dispute Appeal (Future):}
\begin{enumerate}
    \item Party disagrees with arbitrator decision
    \item Party can appeal to higher-level arbitrator or DAO
    \item Appeal requires deposit (prevents frivolous appeals)
    \item Appeal reviewed by different arbitrator or community vote
    \item Final decision cannot be appealed
\end{enumerate}

\textbf{Postconditions:}
\begin{itemize}
    \item Dispute is recorded on-chain
    \item Escrow is marked as disputed
    \item Funds remain locked pending resolution
    \item Resolution decision is transparent and auditable
    \item Funds are distributed according to resolution
    \item Job reaches terminal status
\end{itemize}

\section{Technology Selection Justification}

\subsection{Blockchain Platform: Solana}

Solana was chosen over other blockchain platforms for the following reasons:

\subsubsection{Performance Requirements}

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{High Throughput:} Freelance marketplaces require handling numerous simultaneous transactions (job postings, bids, payments). Solana's 50,000-65,000 TPS capacity far exceeds requirements and supports future scaling.

    \item \textbf{Low Latency:} Sub-second finality provides near-instant user feedback, crucial for good UX. Users can see transaction confirmations within 1-2 seconds instead of waiting 15+ seconds (Ethereum) or minutes (Bitcoin).

    \item \textbf{Cost-Effectiveness:} Transaction fees <\$0.001 enable micro-transactions and make the platform accessible for small projects. On Ethereum, a \$50 job might incur \$25-50 in gas fees, making it economically unviable.
\end{enumerate}

\subsubsection{Technical Advantages}

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Parallel Processing:} Sealevel's ability to execute non-conflicting transactions in parallel means the platform can handle multiple users posting jobs, submitting bids, and releasing payments simultaneously without performance degradation.

    \item \textbf{Predictable Costs:} Unlike Ethereum where gas fees fluctuate wildly (10x-100x during congestion), Solana maintains stable, predictable transaction costs. This allows users to budget accurately.

    \item \textbf{Account Model:} Solana's account-based model with PDAs is well-suited for marketplace data structures. Each job, bid, escrow, and reputation can have its own account with deterministic addressing.
\end{enumerate}

\subsubsection{Developer Experience}

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Anchor Framework:} High-level framework abstracts low-level Solana programming, providing:
    \begin{itemize}
        \item Automatic serialization/deserialization
        \item Built-in security checks
        \item IDL generation for client integration
        \item Testing framework
    \end{itemize}

    \item \textbf{Rust Language:} Memory-safe, performant language ideal for smart contracts. Catches many bugs at compile-time that would be runtime errors in Solidity.

    \item \textbf{Tooling:} Robust development tools including Solana CLI, Solana Playground, and extensive documentation.
\end{enumerate}

\subsubsection{Ecosystem and Adoption}

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Growing User Base:} Increasing number of Solana wallet users (Phantom has 3M+ users)

    \item \textbf{DeFi Integration:} Easy integration with Solana DeFi protocols for future features (lending, staking, DEX integration)

    \item \textbf{NFT Support:} Native NFT support for potential skill certifications, achievement badges

    \item \textbf{Active Community:} Large developer community, regular hackathons, and educational resources
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Smart Contract Framework: Anchor}

Anchor provides significant advantages for Solana development:

\subsubsection{Development Productivity}

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Simplified Account Management:} Anchor handles account validation and initialization automatically. Instead of manually checking account ownership and size, developers use simple macros.

    \item \textbf{Automatic Serialization:} Borsh serialization handled automatically. Developers work with native Rust structs without manual byte manipulation.

    \item \textbf{IDL Generation:} Interface Definition Language file generated automatically, enabling type-safe client integration. Frontend knows exact account structures and function signatures.
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Security Features}

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Built-in Constraints:} Anchor provides security constraints like \texttt{\#[account(mut, constraint = ...)]} that are checked automatically, reducing risk of authorization bugs.

    \item \textbf{Program Derived Addresses:} PDA derivation and verification simplified with \texttt{seeds} and \texttt{bump} parameters.

    \item \textbf{Account Validation:} Type-safe account validation ensures accounts have correct owner, size, and data format.
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Testing and Deployment}

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Testing Framework:} Built-in testing with TypeScript or Rust, supporting unit tests, integration tests, and local validator tests.

    \item \textbf{Deployment Tools:} Simple deployment commands for devnet, testnet, and mainnet with built-in verification.
\end{itemize}

\section{Architecture Design Principles}

\subsection{SOLID Principles Application}

\begin{figure}[h]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.9\textwidth]{figures/solid_principles.png}
\caption{SOLID Principles in System Architecture}
\label{fig:solid_principles}

The application of SOLID principles ensures the codebase is maintainable, testable, and extensible. This section details how each principle is implemented throughout the system.

\subsubsection{Single Responsibility Principle (SRP)}

Each class and module has exactly one reason to change, with clearly defined responsibilities.

\textbf{Implementation Examples:}
\begin{itemize}
    \item \texttt{JobService}: Handles only job-related business logic (create, update, list, filter)
    \item \texttt{BidService}: Manages bidding operations (submit, accept, reject)
    \item \texttt{EscrowService}: Controls payment and fund management (deposit, release, refund)
    \item \texttt{ReputationService}: Calculates and updates reputation scores
    \item \texttt{NotificationService}: Handles event monitoring and user notifications
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Benefits Achieved:}
\begin{itemize}
    \item Easy to locate functionality when debugging
    \item Changes to one feature don't affect others
    \item Clear API boundaries between services
    \item Simplified unit testing with focused test suites
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Open/Closed Principle (OCP)}

The system is designed to be open for extension but closed for modification through strategic use of abstractions and interfaces.

\textbf{Strategy Pattern for Dispute Resolution:}
\begin{lstlisting}[language=TypeScript, caption=Dispute Resolution Strategy]
interface IDisputeStrategy {
    resolve(dispute: Dispute): Promise<Resolution>;
}

class ArbitratorStrategy implements IDisputeStrategy {
    async resolve(dispute: Dispute): Promise<Resolution> {
        // Designated arbitrator makes decision
    }
}

class VotingStrategy implements IDisputeStrategy {
    async resolve(dispute: Dispute): Promise<Resolution> {
        // Community voting determines outcome
    }
}

class AutomatedStrategy implements IDisputeStrategy {
    async resolve(dispute: Dispute): Promise<Resolution> {
        // AI/ML-based automated resolution
    }
}
\end{lstlisting}

\textbf{Provider Pattern for Storage:}
\begin{lstlisting}[language=TypeScript, caption=Storage Provider Abstraction]
interface IStorageProvider {
    upload(file: File): Promise<string>;
    download(hash: string): Promise<File>;
}

class IPFSProvider implements IStorageProvider { }
class ArweaveProvider implements IStorageProvider { }
class LocalProvider implements IStorageProvider { }
\end{lstlisting}

\textbf{Benefits Achieved:}
\begin{itemize}
    \item Add new dispute resolution strategies without modifying existing code
    \item Swap storage providers (IPFS, Arweave) with zero code changes
    \item Extend notification channels (email, SMS, push) easily
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Liskov Substitution Principle (LSP)}

All derived classes can substitute their base classes without breaking functionality.

\textbf{Entity Hierarchy:}
\begin{lstlisting}[language=TypeScript, caption=Entity Base Class]
abstract class BaseEntity {
    id: string;
    createdAt: Date;
    updatedAt: Date;

    validate(): boolean { /* Base validation */ }
}

class Job extends BaseEntity {
    // Job-specific properties
    validate(): boolean {
        return super.validate() && this.budget > 0;
    }
}

class Bid extends BaseEntity {
    // Bid-specific properties
    validate(): boolean {
        return super.validate() && this.amount > 0;
    }
}
\end{lstlisting}

\subsubsection{Interface Segregation Principle (ISP)}

Interfaces are kept small and focused, so clients aren't forced to depend on methods they don't use.

\textbf{Repository Interfaces:}
\begin{lstlisting}[language=TypeScript, caption=Segregated Repository Interfaces]
// Instead of one large IRepository with all methods:
interface IJobRepository {
    createJob(job: Job): Promise<string>;
    findJob(id: string): Promise<Job>;
    listJobs(filter: JobFilter): Promise<Job[]>;
}

interface IBidRepository {
    submitBid(bid: Bid): Promise<string>;
    findBidsForJob(jobId: string): Promise<Bid[]>;
}

interface IEscrowRepository {
    createEscrow(jobId: string, amount: number): Promise<string>;
    releasePayment(escrowId: string): Promise<void>;
}
\end{lstlisting}

\subsubsection{Dependency Inversion Principle (DIP)}

High-level modules depend on abstractions, not concrete implementations. Dependencies are injected rather than created.

\textbf{Dependency Injection Example:}
\begin{lstlisting}[language=TypeScript, caption=Constructor Injection]
class JobService {
    constructor(
        private jobRepo: IJobRepository,
        private notificationService: INotificationService,
        private storageProvider: IStorageProvider
    ) { }

    async createJob(jobData: CreateJobDTO): Promise<Job> {
        // Upload description to storage
        const descHash = await this.storageProvider.upload(
            jobData.description
        );

        // Save job to blockchain
        const job = await this.jobRepo.createJob({
            ...jobData,
            descriptionHash: descHash
        });

        // Notify interested freelancers
        await this.notificationService.notifyNewJob(job);

        return job;
    }
}
\end{lstlisting}

\subsection{Design Patterns}

\subsubsection{Repository Pattern}

Abstracts data access logic from business logic, providing a clean separation between the application and data layers.

\textbf{Benefits:}
\begin{itemize}
    \item Easy to mock repositories for unit testing
    \item Can switch between RPC providers without affecting business logic
    \item Centralized data access logic for consistency
    \item Caching can be implemented transparently in the repository
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Strategy Pattern}

Enables selecting algorithms at runtime without conditional logic.

\textbf{Use Cases:}
\begin{itemize}
    \item Dispute resolution algorithms (arbitrator, voting, automated)
    \item Payment processing (instant, milestone-based, subscription)
    \item Notification delivery (in-app, email, webhook)
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Observer Pattern}

Used for event-driven architecture, particularly in the notification system.

\textbf{Implementation:}
\begin{lstlisting}[language=TypeScript, caption=Event Observer Pattern]
class JobEventObserver {
    private listeners: Map<JobEvent, Function[]> = new Map();

    subscribe(event: JobEvent, callback: Function) {
        if (!this.listeners.has(event)) {
            this.listeners.set(event, []);
        }
        this.listeners.get(event)!.push(callback);
    }

    emit(event: JobEvent, data: any) {
        this.listeners.get(event)?.forEach(cb => cb(data));
    }
}

// Usage
jobObserver.subscribe(JobEvent.NewBid, (bid) => {
    notificationService.notifyClient(bid.jobId, bid);
});
\end{lstlisting}

Used for creating complex objects with varying configurations.

\textbf{Example:}

    pub completed_jobs: u32,        // 4 bytes

    pub bump: u8,                   // 1 byte
}

impl Reputation {
    pub fn average_rating(&self) -> f64 {
        if self.review_count == 0 {
            0.0
        } else {
            (self.total_rating as f64) / (self.review_count as f64)
        }
    }

    pub fn success_rate(&self) -> f64 {
        if self.total_jobs == 0 {
            0.0
        } else {
            (self.completed_jobs as f64) / (self.total_jobs as f64) * 100.0
        }
    }
}

\subsubsection{Submit Bid Instruction}

\begin{lstlisting}[language=Rust, caption=Submit Bid Implementation]
pub fn submit_bid(
    ctx: Context<SubmitBid>,
    proposed_budget: u64,
    proposal: String,
    timeline_days: u16,
    cv_uri: Option<String>,
) -> Result<()> {
    let bid = &mut ctx.accounts.bid;
    let job = &mut ctx.accounts.job;
    let clock = Clock::get()?;

    // Business logic validation
    require!(job.status == JobStatus::Open, ErrorCode::JobNotOpen);
    require!(proposed_budget > 0, ErrorCode::InvalidBudget);
    require!(proposal.len() <= 500, ErrorCode::ProposalTooLong);

    // Anti-sniping: bid must not exceed budget
    require!(
        proposed_budget <= job.budget,
        ErrorCode::BidExceedsBudget
    );

    // Initialize bid account
    bid.job = job.key();
    bid.freelancer = ctx.accounts.freelancer.key();
    bid.proposed_budget = proposed_budget;
    bid.proposal = proposal;
    bid.timeline_days = timeline_days;
    bid.cv_uri = cv_uri.unwrap_or_default();
    bid.status = BidStatus::Pending;
    bid.created_at = clock.unix_timestamp;
    bid.bump = ctx.bumps.bid;

    // Update job metadata
    job.bid_count = job.bid_count.checked_add(1).unwrap();
    job.updated_at = clock.unix_timestamp;

    emit!(BidSubmitted {
        bid: bid.key(),
        job: job.key(),
        freelancer: bid.freelancer,
        proposed_budget,
        timestamp: clock.unix_timestamp,
    });

    Ok(())
}
\end{lstlisting}

\subsubsection{Escrow Management}

\textbf{Deposit Escrow:}
\begin{lstlisting}[language=Rust, caption=Deposit Escrow Implementation]
pub fn deposit_escrow(
    ctx: Context<DepositEscrow>,
    amount: u64
) -> Result<()> {
    let job = &mut ctx.accounts.job;
    let escrow = &mut ctx.accounts.escrow;

    require!(
        job.status == JobStatus::InProgress,
        ErrorCode::InvalidJobStatus
    );
    require!(amount > 0, ErrorCode::InvalidAmount);

    // Transfer SOL to escrow PDA
    let cpi_context = CpiContext::new(
        ctx.accounts.system_program.to_account_info(),
        anchor_lang::system_program::Transfer {
            from: ctx.accounts.client.to_account_info(),
            to: escrow.to_account_info(),
        },
    );
    anchor_lang::system_program::transfer(cpi_context, amount)?;

    escrow.amount = amount;
    escrow.status = EscrowStatus::Held;
    escrow.deposited_at = Clock::get()?.unix_timestamp;

    job.escrow_amount = amount;

    emit!(EscrowDeposited {
        escrow: escrow.key(),
        job: job.key(),
        amount,
        timestamp: Clock::get()?.unix_timestamp,
    });

    Ok(())
}
\end{lstlisting}

\textbf{Release Escrow:}
\begin{lstlisting}[language=Rust, caption=Release Escrow Implementation]
pub fn release_escrow(ctx: Context<ReleaseEscrow>) -> Result<()> {
    let job = &mut ctx.accounts.job;
    let escrow = &mut ctx.accounts.escrow;

    require!(
        job.client == ctx.accounts.client.key(),
        ErrorCode::Unauthorized
    );
    require!(
        escrow.status == EscrowStatus::Held,
        ErrorCode::InvalidEscrowStatus
    );

    // Transfer funds from escrow to freelancer
    let amount = escrow.amount;
    **escrow.to_account_info().try_borrow_mut_lamports()? -= amount;
    **ctx.accounts.freelancer.try_borrow_mut_lamports()? += amount;

    escrow.status = EscrowStatus::Released;
    escrow.released_at = Some(Clock::get()?.unix_timestamp);
    job.status = JobStatus::Completed;

    emit!(EscrowReleased {
        escrow: escrow.key(),
        job: job.key(),
        freelancer: escrow.freelancer,
        amount,
        timestamp: Clock::get()?.unix_timestamp,
    });

    Ok(())
}
\end{lstlisting}

    ): Promise<string> {
        const wallet = this.program.provider.wallet;
        const jobId = Date.now();

        const [jobPda] = await PublicKey.findProgramAddress(
            [
                Buffer.from("job"),
                wallet.publicKey.toBuffer(),
                new BN(jobId).toArrayLike(Buffer, "le", 8)
            ],
            this.program.programId
        );

        const tx = await this.program.methods
            .createJob(
                new BN(jobId),
                title,
                description,
                new BN(budget),
                metadataUri,
                null  // SOL payment
            )
            .accounts({
                job: jobPda,
                client: wallet.publicKey,
                systemProgram: SystemProgram.programId,
            })
            .rpc();

        return tx;
    }

    async listJobs(filter?: JobFilter): Promise<Job[]> {
        const jobs = await this.program.account.job.all();

        return jobs
            .map(j => ({
                pubkey: j.publicKey,
                ...j.account
            }))
            .filter(job => {
                if (filter?.status && job.status !== filter.status)
                    return false;
                if (filter?.minBudget && job.budget < filter.minBudget)
                    return false;
                if (filter?.maxBudget && job.budget > filter.maxBudget)
                    return false;
                return true;
            });
    }
}
\end{lstlisting}

export class RoleDetectionService {
    async detectUserRole(userPublicKey: PublicKey): Promise<UserRole> {
        // Fetch user's reputation account
        const reputation = await this.fetchReputation(userPublicKey);

        if (reputation) {
            // User has reputation, analyze activity
            const score = this.calculateRoleScore(reputation);
            return score.freelancerScore > score.clientScore
                ? UserRole.Freelancer
                : UserRole.Client;
        }

        // Fetch all jobs created by user
        const jobsCreated = await this.fetchJobsByClient(userPublicKey);

        // Fetch all bids submitted by user
        const bidsSubmitted = await this.fetchBidsByFreelancer(
            userPublicKey
        );

        // Decision logic
        if (jobsCreated.length > 0 && bidsSubmitted.length === 0) {
            return UserRole.Client;
        } else if (bidsSubmitted.length > 0 && jobsCreated.length === 0) {
            return UserRole.Freelancer;
        } else if (bidsSubmitted.length > jobsCreated.length) {
            return UserRole.Freelancer;
        } else if (jobsCreated.length > bidsSubmitted.length) {
            return UserRole.Client;
        }

        // Default: show both options
        return UserRole.Unknown;
    }

    private calculateRoleScore(reputation: Reputation): RoleScore {
        return {
            freelancerScore: reputation.completedJobs * 2 +
                           reputation.reviewCount,
            clientScore: reputation.totalJobs - reputation.completedJobs +
                        reputation.reviewCount
        };
    }
}

\begin{lstlisting}[language=TypeScript, caption=Notification Service Implementation]
export class NotificationService {
    private pollInterval: NodeJS.Timeout | null = null;
    private lastCheckedTimestamp: number = Date.now();

    startPolling(userPublicKey: PublicKey, callback: Function) {
        this.pollInterval = setInterval(async () => {
            await this.checkForNewEvents(userPublicKey, callback);
        }, 5000); // Poll every 5 seconds
    }

    private async checkForNewEvents(
        userPublicKey: PublicKey,
        callback: Function
    ) {
        // Fetch recent transactions
        const signatures = await this.connection.getSignaturesForAddress(
            userPublicKey,
            { limit: 10 }
        );

        for (const sig of signatures) {
            if (sig.blockTime && sig.blockTime > this.lastCheckedTimestamp) {
                const tx = await this.connection.getParsedTransaction(
                    sig.signature
                );

                if (tx) {
                    const event = this.parseTransactionForEvents(tx);
                    if (event) {
                        callback(event);
                    }
                }
            }
        }

        this.lastCheckedTimestamp = Date.now();
    }

    stopPolling() {
        if (this.pollInterval) {
            clearInterval(this.pollInterval);
        }
    }
}

\section{Lessons Learned}

\subsection{Technical Lessons}

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Account Size Matters:} On-chain storage is expensive. Strategic use of IPFS for large data and careful data structure design can reduce costs by 40-60\%.

    \item \textbf{PDAs are Powerful:} Program Derived Addresses provide elegant solution for deterministic account generation, access control, and program-signed transactions.

    \item \textbf{Test Early and Often:} Comprehensive test suite catches bugs before deployment. Smart contract bugs are difficult to fix post-deployment, making upfront testing critical.

    \item \textbf{RPC Reliability is Key:} Frontend performance heavily depends on RPC provider reliability. Implement caching, retry logic, and fallback providers.

    \item \textbf{Events Enable Observability:} Emitting events from smart contracts enables off-chain indexing, notifications, and analytics. Essential for user experience.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Architectural Lessons}

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{SOLID Principles Apply:} Traditional software engineering principles translate well to blockchain development. Investment in architecture pays dividends in maintainability.

    \item \textbf{Separation of Concerns:} Clean separation between presentation, business logic, and infrastructure layers simplifies testing and enables parallel development.

    \item \textbf{Interfaces Enable Flexibility:} Abstracting behind interfaces (repositories, services) allows swapping implementations without affecting dependent code.

    \item \textbf{Design Patterns Reduce Complexity:} Strategic use of design patterns (Strategy, Repository, Observer) manages complexity in large codebases.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{User Experience Lessons}

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Cryptocurrency UX is Challenging:} Users struggle with wallet setup, transaction signing, and gas fee concepts. Extensive education and clear UI guidance essential.

    \item \textbf{Speed Matters:} Instant confirmation and fast finality significantly improve user satisfaction. Solana's speed advantage is noticeable and valued by users.

    \item \textbf{Cost is Compelling:} Near-zero transaction fees eliminate major barrier to adoption. Users immediately appreciate economic benefits.

    \item \textbf{Transparency Builds Trust:} Users value ability to verify transactions on blockchain. Transparency is powerful differentiator vs traditional platforms.
\end{enumerate}

\section{UC-01: Create Job Posting}

\textbf{Actor:} Client

\textbf{Preconditions:}
\begin{itemize}
    \item User has connected Solana wallet
    \item User has sufficient SOL for transaction fees
    \item User is detected as Client role
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Main Flow:}
\begin{enumerate}
    \item Client navigates to "Create Job" page
    \item System displays job creation form
    \item Client enters job details:
    \begin{itemize}
        \item Title (required, max 100 characters)
        \item Description (required, max 500 characters)
        \item Budget in SOL (required, >0)
        \item Deadline date (optional)
        \item Skills required (optional)
        \item Additional metadata URI (optional, stored on IPFS)
    \end{itemize}
    \item Client reviews entered information
    \item Client clicks "Create Job" button
    \item System validates all inputs
    \item System prompts wallet for transaction approval
    \item Client approves transaction in wallet
    \item System submits transaction to Solana blockchain
    \item System waits for transaction confirmation
    \item System displays success message with job link
    \item System navigates to job detail page
\end{enumerate}

\begin{enumerate}
    \item System displays error message indicating which fields are invalid
    \item System highlights problematic fields
    \item User corrects errors and resubmits
\end{enumerate}

\textit{A2. User Rejects Transaction:}
\begin{enumerate}
    \item User clicks "Reject" in wallet
    \item System displays message "Transaction cancelled by user"
    \item Job creation form remains filled for retry
\end{enumerate}

\textit{A3. Transaction Fails:}
\begin{enumerate}
    \item Blockchain returns error (insufficient funds, network issue, etc.)
    \item System displays specific error message
    \item System offers retry option
\end{enumerate}

\textbf{Postconditions:}
\begin{itemize}
    \item New Job account created on Solana blockchain
    \item Job visible in public job listing
    \item JobCreated event emitted
    \item Client receives notification of successful creation
\end{itemize}

    \item System confirms transaction success
    \item System updates escrow status to "Held"
    \item System notifies freelancer to begin work
\end{enumerate}

\textbf{Alternative Flows:}

\begin{enumerate}
    \item Wallet rejects due to insufficient balance
    \item System displays error with required amount
    \item Client must add SOL to wallet
    \item Client retries deposit
\end{enumerate}

\textbf{Postconditions:}
\begin{itemize}
    \item Escrow account holds funds
    \item Job escrow\_amount updated
    \item EscrowDeposited event emitted
    \item Freelancer can begin work
    \item Funds locked until job completion or dispute

\begin{enumerate}

    \item System displays dispute form
    \item User enters:
    \begin{itemize}
        \item Dispute reason (required)
        \item Description of issue (required, max 1000 characters)
        \item Evidence IPFS hashes (optional)
    \end{itemize}
    \item User clicks "Submit Dispute" button
    \item System creates dispute account
    \item System locks escrow pending resolution
    \item System notifies other party
    \item Other party can respond with counter-evidence
    \item System applies resolution strategy:
    \begin{itemize}
        \item If arbitrator assigned: waits for arbitrator decision
        \item If voting enabled: community votes weighted by reputation
        \item If automated: analyzes evidence and patterns
    \end{itemize}
    \item System applies resolution decision
    \item System distributes escrow accordingly
    \item System updates job status
    \item Both parties notified of outcome
\end{enumerate}

\textbf{Alternative Flows:}

\begin{enumerate}

    \item Either party submits agreed resolution
    \item Other party confirms agreement
    \item System applies resolution
    \item Dispute closed without formal arbitration
\end{enumerate}

\textit{A2. Appeal Decision:}
\begin{enumerate}
    \item Party disagrees with resolution
    \item Party submits appeal with new evidence
    \item Higher-tier resolution strategy applied
    \item Final decision made
\end{enumerate}

\textbf{Postconditions:}
\begin{itemize}
    \item Dispute resolved
    \item Escrow distributed per resolution
    \item DisputeResolved event emitted
    \item Job status updated
    \item Dispute record permanent on blockchain
    \item May affect reputation of involved parties
\end{itemize}

async function loadTest() {
    const concurrentUsers = [10, 50, 100, 500];

    for (const users of concurrentUsers) {
        console.log(`Testing with ${users} concurrent users...`);

        const start = Date.now();
        const promises = [];

        const results = await Promise.allSettled(promises);

            successful,

        });
    }
}

    \item Review each bid:
    \begin{itemize}
        \item Freelancer's proposal
        \item Proposed budget
        \item Timeline estimate
        \item Freelancer's reputation
    \end{itemize}
    \item Click "View Profile" to see freelancer details
    \item Click "Accept" on preferred bid
    \item Confirm acceptance and approve transaction
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Managing Escrow}

\textbf{Depositing Escrow:}
\begin{enumerate}
    \item After accepting bid, you'll be prompted to deposit escrow
    \item Review escrow amount (matches accepted bid)
    \item Click "Deposit Escrow"
    \item Approve transaction in wallet
    \item Funds locked until work approved
\end{enumerate}

\textbf{Approving Work:}
\begin{enumerate}
    \item Receive notification when work submitted
    \item Navigate to job page
    \item Click "View Deliverables"
    \item Download and review submitted work
    \item If satisfactory:
    \begin{itemize}
        \item Click "Approve Work"
        \item Payment automatically released
    \end{itemize}
    \item If revisions needed:
    \begin{itemize}
        \item Click "Request Revision"
        \item Describe needed changes
    \end{itemize}
\end{enumerate}

\section{Troubleshooting}

\subsection{Common Issues}

\textbf{Transaction Failed:}
\begin{itemize}
    \item Ensure sufficient SOL for transaction fees
    \item Check network status at \texttt{status.solana.com}
    \item Try again after a few moments
    \item Switch RPC endpoint if persistent
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Wallet Won't Connect:}
\begin{itemize}
    \item Refresh page and try again
    \item Check wallet extension is enabled
    \item Try different wallet
    \item Clear browser cache
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Job/Bid Not Appearing:}
\begin{itemize}
    \item Wait for transaction confirmation (1-2 seconds)
    \item Refresh page
    \item Check blockchain explorer for transaction
\end{itemize}
